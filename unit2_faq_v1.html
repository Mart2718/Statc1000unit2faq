Basic Probability

S8 · 4 questions

The answer

The keyword in the problem tells you which rule to use:

"OR" → Addition Rule. P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B). You subtract the overlap to avoid double-counting. If A and B are mutually exclusive (can't happen at the same time), the overlap is zero: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).

"AND" → Multiplication Rule. P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B|A). If A and B are independent, P(B|A) = P(B), so it simplifies to P(A) × P(B).

🧠 Memory anchor: OR = more ways to succeed → add. AND = both must happen → multiply. "And" is more restrictive — the probability shrinks when both events are required simultaneously.
⚠️ Common mistake: Forgetting to subtract the overlap in "or" problems when events are NOT mutually exclusive. If a student can be both left-handed AND a Biology major, counting them under "left-handed" AND again under "Biology major" double-counts. Always ask: "Can both happen at once?" If yes, subtract P(A and B).

The answer

They are completely different concepts that happen to sound similar.

Mutually exclusive (disjoint): The two events cannot happen at the same time. P(A and B) = 0. Knowing A happened tells you B definitely did NOT happen. Example: a coin flip landing heads AND tails simultaneously — impossible.

Independent: Whether one event happens has no effect on the probability of the other. P(A|B) = P(A). Knowing B gives you no new information about A. Example: flipping a coin twice — the first flip doesn't affect the second.

Side-by-side: Mutually Exclusive vs Independent

Both happen at once?Impossible (P(A∩B) = 0)Possible (P(A∩B) = P(A)·P(B))
Does one affect the other?Yes — if A happens, B can'tNo — neither affects the other
Check usingP(A and B) = 0?P(A|B) = P(A)? (or P(A)·P(B) = P(A∩B)?)
ExampleRolling a 2 and rolling a 5 on one dieRolling a die, then flipping a coin
Critical insight: If two events are mutually exclusive AND both have nonzero probability, they CANNOT be independent. Knowing A happened makes P(B) = 0 — that's the opposite of independence.

The answer

The formula: P(A | B) = P(A and B) / P(B)

Denominator: always the "given" condition — the event after the vertical bar "|". This is B. It becomes your new restricted sample space. In a contingency table: use the row total or column total for B.

Numerator: the joint event — both A AND B occurring simultaneously. In a contingency table: find the cell where A and B intersect.

Reading "P(A|B)": "Given that B happened, what is the probability that A also happened?" You're narrowing your universe down to only the cases where B occurred, then asking how many of those also have A.

🧠 Contingency table shortcut: "Given" tells you which row/column to zoom into. Once you're inside that row or column, the conditional probability = (cell count) ÷ (that row/column total). You never go back to the grand total.
⚠️ Common mistake: Dividing by the grand total instead of the "given" row/column total. P(A|B) restricts you to B's universe — its denominator is P(B) or the row/column total for B, not the full table total.

The answer

When you sample without replacement, each draw technically changes the remaining pool — so the trials aren't perfectly independent. However, if your sample is tiny relative to the population, removing one person barely changes the probabilities for the next draw.

Example: A town has 100,000 people. You remove 50 for your sample. The probability of any characteristic changes by at most 50/100,000 = 0.05% — essentially negligible. The trials behave as if independent.

The 5% condition (n/N < 0.05) formalizes this: as long as your sample is less than 5% of the population, the dependence is small enough to ignore and the binomial/independence-based formulas remain valid.

🧠 Analogy: Taking a cup of water from the ocean doesn't meaningfully change the ocean's salinity. Taking a bucket from a bathtub does. The 5% rule asks: are you drawing from an "ocean" (huge population) or a "bathtub" (small population)?

Expected Value & Binomial Distribution

S9 · S10 · 8 questions

The answer

Expected value (EV) is the long-run average outcome over many, many repetitions of the same random experiment. It is a weighted average of all possible outcomes, where each outcome is weighted by its probability.

Yes, EV can be a value that never actually occurs. The classic example: "expected number of children in a family" might be 2.3 — no family has 2.3 children, but if you average across thousands of families, you approach 2.3. The EV is a theoretical center of the distribution, not a prediction for any single trial.

Interpretation: "In the long run, if this experiment were repeated many times, the average outcome would be [EV]."

🧠 Raffle example: If EV = −$2.50 for a raffle ticket buyer, it means on average, each ticket buyer loses $2.50 per ticket over many plays. No individual play results in losing exactly $2.50 — you either win a prize or get nothing. But the long-run average loss per ticket is $2.50.
⚠️ Common mistake: Interpreting EV as "the most likely outcome." It's not the mode — it's the balance point of the distribution. A coin flip has EV = 0.5 heads, but you can only get 0 or 1 on any single flip.
Try the Expected Value Explorer — watch the long-run average settle in real time

The answer

All probabilities in a valid discrete distribution must sum to exactly 1.0. This gives you a simple equation to solve for any missing value:

Missing P = 1 − (sum of all other given probabilities)

Once you have the missing P, check: is 0 ≤ missing P ≤ 1? If not, there's an error in the given probabilities.

After completing the table, proceed to expected value: EV = Σ x · P(x) — multiply each outcome by its probability and sum all products.

🧠 Always verify validity first: (1) All P(x) values are between 0 and 1. (2) All P(x) values sum to exactly 1. If either condition fails, the table is not a valid probability distribution.

The answer

Formula: EV = Σ x · P(x)

Step 1: Write out every possible outcome value (x) and its probability P(x) in a table.

Step 2: For each row, multiply x × P(x). Add a new column for these products.

Step 3: Sum all the x · P(x) products. That sum is the expected value.

Step 4: Write the interpretation: "In the long run, the average [outcome variable] per [trial] is [EV] [units]."

Raffle example: You pay $5 for a ticket. Prize: $100 (probability 0.02), $0 (probability 0.98). Write outcomes from the buyer's perspective as net gain/loss: x = $95 (win $100, paid $5) with P = 0.02; x = −$5 (lose entry fee) with P = 0.98. EV = (95)(0.02) + (−5)(0.98) = 1.90 − 4.90 = −$3.00. On average, each ticket buyer loses $3.00.
🧠 Buyer vs. seller: Write outcomes from one consistent perspective (usually the buyer). If EV is negative for the buyer → buyer loses in the long run → seller profits. Most real-world games and raffles have negative EV for players by design.

The answer

The standard deviation (σ) of a probability distribution measures how spread out the outcomes are around the expected value — how much you should expect a typical outcome to deviate from the expected value.

Large σ: outcomes vary widely; individual results are unpredictable and often far from the EV. High variability — high risk.

Small σ: outcomes cluster tightly near the EV; individual results are predictable and consistent. Low variability — low risk.

Template: "The standard deviation of [σ units] means that on any given [trial], the [outcome] typically varies from the expected value of [EV] by about [σ] [units]."

🧠 Real-world connection: Insurance companies care deeply about the standard deviation of payouts — they want the variance in claims to be predictable. A game with high EV and low σ is more reliably profitable than one with the same EV and high σ. The σ measures risk; the EV measures the long-run reward.

The answer

Write one sentence for each condition — this is what full credit requires:

B — Binary: Each trial results in exactly one of two outcomes: success or failure (yes/no, heads/tails, defective/not defective). "Each [trial] can result in either [success] or [not success]."

I — Independent: The outcome of one trial does not affect any other. "Whether [one trial] succeeds does not change the probability of [another trial] succeeding" (or: the 5% condition is met).

N — Number fixed: The total number of trials (n) is set in advance. "There are exactly n = [___] trials."

S — Same probability: The probability of success (p) is identical for every trial. "The probability of [success] is p = [___] for each trial."

🧠 Exam tip: Don't just list the BINS acronym — write a sentence for EACH condition in the context of the actual problem. "B: Binary. ✓" earns no credit. "B: Each free throw results in either a make (success) or a miss (failure)." earns full credit.
⚠️ Common error: Confusing "binary outcome" with "two groups." Binary means each individual trial has exactly two possible outcomes — it doesn't mean the whole experiment has two groups. A student either passes or fails each question → binary. The class has 30 students → that's n, not binary vs. not binary.

The answer

Binomial counts must be whole numbers — you can't make 9.9 shots. When your fence is a decimal, you must convert it to a whole number, but the direction depends on which side you're on:

Lower fence = 9.9: Values below 9.9 are unusual. The lowest whole number you can be and still be unusual is 9 (since 10 ≥ 9.9, so 10 is inside the fence). → "9 or fewer is unusually low."

Upper fence = 18.1: Values above 18.1 are unusual. The lowest whole number that is above 18.1 is 19 (since 18 ≤ 18.1, so 18 is inside the fence). → "19 or more is unusually high."

Direction rule: Lower fence — round DOWN to find the highest "unusual" value (values BELOW the fence). Upper fence — round UP to find the lowest "unusual" value (values ABOVE the fence). Never use the rounded fence value itself — check which side of the decimal it falls on.
⚠️ The common error: Rounding 9.9 up to 10 and concluding "10 or fewer is unusual." But 10 is NOT below 9.9 — it's above it. 10 falls inside the normal range. Only 9 and below are unusual. When in doubt, ask: "Is this value actually below (or above) the fence?" before concluding unusual.

The answer

The unshaded region represents the complement — the probability that the outcome falls outside the shaded area. Shaded + unshaded always = 1.0 (100% of the probability).

🧠 In Art of Stat: You don't need to manually shade — pick the correct inequality symbol from the dropdown. "P(X < value)" → choose "<". "P(X > value)" → choose ">". The tool shades automatically and gives you the exact probability.
Try the Z-Score Explorer — practice reading left, right, and middle regions

The answer

Use the probability directly when the question asks for a left-tail ("less than") area — Art of Stat and z-tables naturally give you left-tail probabilities.

Subtract from 1 when the question asks for a right-tail ("greater than," "more than," "above") area: P(X > value) = 1 − P(X < value).

For "between" two values: P(a < X < b) = P(X < b) − P(X < a). Compute the left-tail probability for the upper bound, then subtract the left-tail probability for the lower bound.

Quick check: Is the shaded area to the LEFT of your value? → Use directly. To the RIGHT? → Subtract from 1. In the MIDDLE? → Subtract the smaller left-area from the larger left-area.
🧠 Art of Stat does this automatically — just pick the right inequality symbol. The subtraction is built into the tool. But you need to understand the logic so you can verify the answer makes sense. A probability of 0.97 for a "greater than" question on a value far above the mean should raise a flag — that should be a small number, not 0.97.

The answer

Use the Empirical Rule (68–95–99.7) only when the problem involves values that are exactly 1, 2, or 3 standard deviations from the mean — and even then, only as a quick approximation.

Empirical Rule probabilities: within 1σ → ≈68%; within 2σ → ≈95%; within 3σ → ≈99.7%.

Use Art of Stat for everything else — any decimal z-score, any specific cutoff value, any exact calculation. Art of Stat gives you precise probabilities without rounding.

Signal words for Empirical Rule: "within 1 standard deviation," "between μ−2σ and μ+2σ," "within three standard deviations." If the problem specifies a specific numeric value that doesn't land exactly on 1, 2, or 3 SDs from the mean → use Art of Stat.

🧠 When in doubt, use Art of Stat. The Empirical Rule is a useful approximation for quick mental math — it is not a substitute for exact calculation on an exam problem that gives you specific values. Always prefer the precise tool unless the problem explicitly invites an approximation.

The answer

Step 1 — Identify the parameters: What is μ (mean) and σ (standard deviation) for this distribution? These come directly from the problem.

Step 2 — Identify what you're finding: Is the problem asking "P(X < ___)", "P(X > ___)", or "P(a < X < b)"? Read carefully.

Step 3 — Open Art of Stat: Go to Distributions → Normal Distribution → "Find Probability" tab. Enter your μ and σ.

Step 4 — Enter the boundary value: Type the specific x-value from the problem. Select the correct inequality direction. Click calculate.

Step 5 — Interpret in context: "The probability that [variable in context] is [greater/less] than [value] is approximately [result]."

⚠️ Most common Art of Stat error: Entering the z-score into the "x" field instead of the original data value. Art of Stat handles the z-score conversion internally when you enter μ and σ. Only enter raw x-values, not z-scores, in the normal distribution calculator — unless the problem gives you a standardized distribution (μ=0, σ=1).

Central Limit Theorem

S12 · 9 questions

⭐ The Most Important Question in S12 — Memorize This

How do I tell whether a problem is about ONE individual or a SAMPLE of n people — and why does that change everything?

Individual observation

Use σ (population SD)
Signal words: "a randomly selected person," "one student," "a single value," "an individual"
Formula: z = (x − μ) / σ

Sample of n values

Use σ/√n (standard error)
Signal words: "a sample of n," "the average of n," "the mean of a group of n"
Formula: z = (x̄ − μ) / (σ/√n)

🧠 The single fastest trigger: Does the problem mention a sample of n people (or n observations) and ask about their average or mean? → Use σ/√n. Is it asking about the probability for just one randomly selected person? → Use σ as-is.

The answer

It comes from the mathematics of variance. If you sum n independent values, the variance of the sum grows as n (not n²). When you take the average (divide the sum by n), the variance becomes σ²/n — so the standard deviation of the average is σ/√n.

The intuitive explanation: Larger samples produce more accurate estimates — your sample mean is less likely to be far from the true population mean. This is why sampling distributions get "skinnier" as n grows. But the improvement follows the square root — doubling accuracy requires quadrupling n, not just doubling it.

Example: σ = 10. With n=4: SE = 10/√4 = 5. With n=100: SE = 10/√100 = 1. With n=400: SE = 10/√400 = 0.5. Each time you quadruple n, the SE halves.

🧠 Key insight: The distribution of individual values has spread σ. The distribution of sample means has spread σ/√n — always narrower than individual values, and gets narrower as n grows. This is exactly what the CLT guarantees: large samples → sample means cluster tightly around the true population mean.

The answer

🧠 The mean is already given: For both types, the center of the sampling distribution equals the population parameter — μ for means, p for proportions. You never need to calculate the center. You only need to calculate the standard error (spread), then use the normal distribution for probabilities.
⚠️ Riley's confusion resolved: When a problem says "the true proportion is p = 0.55 and n = 60," the mean of the sampling distribution is μp̂ = 0.55 (not 0.55 × 60 = 33). That product (33) would be the expected number of successes in a binomial context — a different question. For CLT for proportions, keep everything in proportion units (0 to 1).

The answer

The n ≥ 30 rule is a guideline for populations that are not normally distributed. Here is the complete picture:

If the underlying population is already normally distributed: The sampling distribution of x̄ is normal for any sample size — even n = 2 or n = 5. You don't need n ≥ 30 because the normal shape is inherited directly.

If the population is skewed or non-normal: The n ≥ 30 guideline applies. For larger n, the distribution of sample means becomes approximately normal regardless of the population shape (this is the core CLT guarantee).

For proportions: The condition is np ≥ 10 AND n(1−p) ≥ 10 — not n ≥ 30. With very small or very large p, you need a larger n to satisfy this.

Complete conditions checklist for CLT:
Means: (a) Population is normal → any n works. OR (b) n ≥ 30 → works regardless of population shape.
Proportions: np ≥ 10 AND n(1−p) ≥ 10 (i.e., you expect at least 10 successes AND 10 failures).

The answer

"Within 10% of the true proportion p" means the sample proportion p̂ falls in the range: p − 0.10 to p + 0.10. This is a "between" probability.

Setup: P(p − 0.10 < p̂ < p + 0.10)

Step 1: Plug in the actual value of p. Example: p = 0.55 → bounds are 0.45 and 0.65.

Step 2: Calculate the standard error: σp̂ = √(p(1−p)/n).

Step 3: In Art of Stat, use Normal Distribution with mean = p and SD = σp̂. Enter the "between" bounds (0.45 and 0.65 in the example).

🧠 "More than 10% away" is the complement: P(p̂ < p − 0.10 or p̂ > p + 0.10) = 1 − P(within 10%). Calculate the within-10% probability first, then subtract from 1.
⚠️ Common error: Interpreting "within 10%" as "within 10 percentage points on one side only." It is symmetric — 10% below AND 10% above the true p. Both bounds are required, and the probability covers the middle region of the sampling distribution.

The answer

Ask these three questions in order — stop when you have a match:

Q1: Am I counting discrete successes in a fixed number of trials? (BINS conditions apply?) → Binomial.

Q2: Am I finding the probability for one individual value from a continuous distribution? ("A randomly selected person…") → Normal Distribution with the original μ and σ.

Q3: Am I finding the probability for a sample mean or sample proportion from a group of n? ("A sample of n…," "the average of n…") → CLT — use σ/√n (means) or √(p(1−p)/n) (proportions) as your standard deviation.

The one-sentence rule: "Count of successes → Binomial. One person → Normal with σ. Group average/proportion → CLT with σ/√n."
🧠 Use the decision tree at the top of this page — it walks you through exactly these three questions interactively. When you land on a result, it shows you the formulas and opens Art of Stat.

The answer

Relationship: The standard error is the standard deviation of the sampling distribution — it describes how much sample means (or proportions) vary from sample to sample. It is always smaller than the population SD by a factor of √n.

Population SD (σ) = how much individual values vary from the mean. Standard Error (σ/√n) = how much sample means vary from sample to sample. As n grows, SE shrinks → larger samples give more consistent estimates.

To stay organized — use a scratch formula template every time:

① Identify: "Individual or sample?" ② Write μ (same as population mean or p). ③ Calculate SE = σ/√n or √(p(1−p)/n). ④ Enter μ and SE into Art of Stat as the new parameters. ⑤ Enter your boundary value and inequality. ⑥ Write context sentence.

🧠 Write the sampling distribution parameters before touching Art of Stat: μx̄ = ___ and σx̄ = σ/√n = ___/√___ = ___. This two-second step prevents you from accidentally using σ instead of σ/√n — the most common CLT mistake on exams.
⚠️ Most frequent CLT error on exams: Entering the population σ directly into Art of Stat when the problem is about a sample mean. You must compute σ/√n FIRST, then enter that result as the standard deviation in Art of Stat's normal distribution calculator.

The answer

Partially — but not entirely. Here's the precise distinction:

What the CLT guarantees: The distribution of sample means becomes approximately normal as n grows, regardless of the original population's shape. For questions about sample means, you can use normal distribution methods even if the original data is skewed.

What the CLT does NOT change: The original population mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ) — these are still what you use as inputs for the CLT formulas. The CLT changes the shape of the sampling distribution, not the parameters it's centered on.

So: when working with a sample mean, yes, treat the sampling distribution as normal. But you still need μ and σ from the original population — the CLT doesn't make them disappear, it just lets you use normal probability tools on top of them.

Key analogy: The original distribution is like the personality of each individual coin. The CLT says: when you average 100 coin flips, the result looks bell-shaped regardless of the coin's individual behavior. You still need to know the coin's average (μ) and variability (σ) — those feed the CLT. The CLT just guarantees the shape of what comes out.

The answer

For CLT problems, use the Normal Distribution tool — not a separate CLT calculator. The key is entering the sampling distribution parameters (not the raw population parameters):

CLT for Means: Mean = μ (population mean), SD = σ/√n (standard error). Enter x̄ (the boundary sample mean) as your value.

CLT for Proportions: Mean = p (true proportion), SD = √(p(1−p)/n) (standard error). Enter p̂ (the boundary sample proportion) as your value.

Path: Distributions → Normal Distribution → "Find Probability" tab → Enter mean and SD as above → Enter your boundary value and inequality → Calculate.

The single most important entry: In the "SD" field, do NOT enter σ or σ² directly. Enter σ/√n (for means) or √(p(1-p)/n) (for proportions) — the computed standard error. This is what distinguishes a CLT problem from a plain normal distribution problem in Art of Stat.
🧠 Double-check: Before clicking calculate, confirm your SD entry is smaller than the original σ. If σ = 15 and n = 36, your SE = 15/6 = 2.5. If your SD field shows 15, you forgot to divide by √n.
